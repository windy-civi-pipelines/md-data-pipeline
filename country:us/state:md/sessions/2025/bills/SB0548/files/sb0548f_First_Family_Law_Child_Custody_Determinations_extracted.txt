By: Senator James
Introduced and read first time: January 23, 2025
Assigned to: Judicial Proceedings
A BILL ENTITLED
1 AN ACT concerning
2 Family Law – Child Custody – Determinations
3 FOR the purpose of authorizing the court, in determining legal and physical custody in
4 certain child custody proceedings, to consider certain factors; authorizing the court
5 to modify a child custody or visitation order under certain circumstances; and
6 generally relating to child custody and visitation.
7 BY repealing and reenacting, without amendments,
8 Article – Family Law
9 Section 9–101, 9–101.1, and 9–101.2
10 Annotated Code of Maryland
11 (2019 Replacement Volume and 2024 Supplement)
12 BY adding to
13 Article – Family Law
14 Section 9–201 and 9–202 to be under the new subtitle “Subtitle 2. Legal and Physical
15 Custody – Judicial Determinations”
16 Annotated Code of Maryland
17 (2019 Replacement Volume and 2024 Supplement)
18 SECTION 1. BE IT ENACTED BY THE GENERAL ASSEMBLY OF MARYLAND,
19 That the Laws of Maryland read as follows:
20 Article – Family Law
21 9–101.
22 (a) In any custody or visitation proceeding, if the court has reasonable grounds to
23 believe that a child has been abused or neglected by a party to the proceeding, the court
24 shall determine whether abuse or neglect is likely to occur if custody or visitation rights
25 are granted to the party.
EXPLANATION: CAPITALS INDICATE MATTER ADDED TO EXISTING LAW.
[Brackets] indicate matter deleted from exist in g l aw . *sb0548*
2 SENATE BILL 548
1 (b) Unless the court specifically finds that there is no likelihood of further child
2 abuse or neglect by the party, the court shall deny custody or visitation rights to that party,
3 except that the court may approve a supervised visitation arrangement that assures the
4 safety and the physiological, psychological, and emotional well–being of the child.
5 9–101.1.
6 (a) In this section, “abuse” has the meaning stated in § 4–501 of this article.
7 (b) In a custody or visitation proceeding, the court shall consider, when deciding
8 custody or visitation issues, evidence of abuse by a party against:
9 (1) the other parent of the party’s child;
10 (2) the party’s spouse; or
11 (3) any child residing within the party’s household, including a child other
12 than the child who is the subject of the custody or visitation proceeding.
13 (c) If the court finds that a party has committed abuse against the other parent
14 of the party’s child, the party’s spouse, or any child residing within the party’s household,
15 the court shall make arrangements for custody or visitation that best protect:
16 (1) the child who is the subject of the proceeding; and
17 (2) the victim of the abuse.
18 9–101.2.
19 (a) Except as provided in subsection (b) of this section, unless good cause for the
20 award of custody or visitation is shown by clear and convincing evidence, a court may not
21 award custody of a child or visitation with a child:
22 (1) to a parent who has been found by a court of this State to be guilty of
23 first degree or second degree murder of the other parent of the child, another child of the
24 parent, or any family member residing in the household of either parent of the child; or
25 (2) to a parent who has been found by a court of any state or of the United
26 States to be guilty of a crime that, if committed in this State, would be first degree murder
27 or second degree murder of the other parent of the child, another child of the parent, or any
28 family member residing in the household of either parent of the child.
29 (b) If it is in the best interest of the child, the court may approve a supervised
30 visitation arrangement that assures the safety and the physiological, psychological, and
31 emotional well–being of the child.

3 (A) SUBJECT TO THE PROVISIONS OF §§ 9–101, 9–101.1, AND 9–101.2 OF
4 THIS TITLE, IN DETERMINING WHAT LEGAL CUSTODY AND PHYSICAL CUSTODY IS IN
5 THE BEST INTEREST OF A CHILD, THE COURT MAY CONSIDER THE FOLLOWING
6 FACTORS:
7 (1) STABILITY AND THE FORESEEABLE HEALTH AND WELFARE OF THE
8 CHILD;
9 (2) FREQUENT, REGULAR, AND CONTINUING CONTACT WITH PARENTS
10 WHO CAN ACT IN THE CHILD’S BEST INTEREST;
11 (3) WHETHER AND HOW PARENTS WHO DO NOT LIVE TOGETHER WILL
12 SHARE THE RIGHTS AND RESPONSIBILITIES OF RAISING THE CHILD;
13 (4) THE CHILD’S RELATIONSHIP WITH EACH PARENT, ANY SIBLINGS,
14 OTHER RELATIVES, AND INDIVIDUALS WHO ARE OR MAY BECOME IMPORTANT IN THE
15 CHILD’S LIFE;
16 (5) THE CHILD’S PHYSICAL AND EMOTIONAL SECURITY AND
17 PROTECTION FROM EXPOSURE TO CONFLICT AND VIOLENCE;
18 (6) THE CHILD’S DEVELOPMENTAL NEEDS, INCLUDING PHYSICAL
19 SAFETY, EMOTIONAL SECURITY, POSITIVE SELF–IMAGE, INTERPERSONAL SKILLS,
20 AND INTELLECTUAL AND COGNITIVE GROWTH;
21 (7) THE DAY–TO–DAY NEEDS OF THE CHILD, INCLUDING EDUCATION,
22 SOCIALIZATION, CULTURE AND RELIGION, FOOD, SHELTER, CLOTHING, AND
23 MENTAL AND PHYSICAL HEALTH;
24 (8) HOW TO:
25 (I) PLACE THE CHILD’S NEEDS ABOVE THE PARENTS’ NEEDS;
26 (II) PROTECT THE CHILD FROM THE NEGATIVE EFFECTS OF ANY
27 CONFLICT BETWEEN THE PARENTS; AND
28 (III) MAINTAIN THE CHILD’S RELATIONSHIP WITH THE PARENTS,
29 SIBLINGS, OTHER RELATIVES, OR OTHER INDIVIDUALS WHO HAVE OR LIKELY MAY
30 HAVE A SIGNIFICANT RELATIONSHIP WITH THE CHILD;

1 (9) THE AGE OF THE CHILD;
2 (10) ANY MILITARY DEPLOYMENT OF A PARENT AND ITS EFFECT, IF
3 ANY, ON THE PARENT–CHILD RELATIONSHIP;
4 (11) ANY PRIOR COURT ORDERS OR AGREEMENTS;
5 (12) EACH PARENT’S ROLE AND TASKS RELATED TO THE CHILD AND
6 HOW, IF AT ALL, THOSE ROLES AND TASKS HAVE CHANGED;
7 (13) THE LOCATION OF EACH PARENT’S HOME AS IT RELATES TO THE
8 PARENT’S ABILITY TO COORDINATE PARENTING TIME, SCHOOL, AND ACTIVITIES;
9 (14) THE PARENTS’ RELATIONSHIP WITH EACH OTHER, INCLUDING:
10 (I) HOW THEY COMMUNICATE WITH EACH OTHER;
11 (II) WHETHER THEY CAN CO–PARENT WITHOUT DISRUPTING
12 THE CHILD’S SOCIAL AND SCHOOL LIFE; AND
13 (III) HOW THE PARENTS WILL RESOLVE ANY DISPUTES IN THE
14 FUTURE WITHOUT THE NEED FOR COURT INTERVENTION;
15 (15) THE CHILD’S PREFERENCE, IF AGE–APPROPRIATE; AND
16 (16) ANY OTHER FACTOR THAT THE COURT CONSIDERS APPROPRIATE
17 IN DETERMINING HOW BEST TO SERVE THE PHYSICAL, DEVELOPMENTAL, AND
18 EMOTIONAL NEEDS OF THE CHILD.
19 (B) THE COURT SHALL ARTICULATE ITS FINDINGS OF FACT ON THE RECORD
20 OR IN A WRITTEN OPINION, INCLUDING THE CONSIDERATION OF EACH FACTOR
21 LISTED IN SUBSECTION (A) OF THIS SECTION AND ANY OTHER FACTOR THAT THE
22 COURT CONSIDERED.

29 ORDER IS IN THE BEST INTEREST OF THE CHILD.
